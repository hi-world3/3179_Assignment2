Victoria's Population Pulse: A Story of Births and Deaths

This visualisation explores how population changes due to births and deaths have manifested across Victoria's Local Government Areas (LGAs) from 2011 to 2023. We will examine which areas are growing naturally, which are aging, and explore the potential reasons why through the data.

Where is the Population Growing or Declining?

The map below illustrates the 'natural increase' (total births minus total deaths) for each LGA in Victoria. Darker blue areas indicate natural growth where births outnumber deaths, while darker red signifies the opposite. Use the slider to explore these changes over the past decade.

Key Observations from the Data

The 2023 Snapshot: A City Divided

In 2023, a clear pattern emerges across Greater Melbourne. The metropolitan fringe areas, particularly in the fast-growing western and northern corridors, exhibit a high natural increase. This is likely linked to housing affordability, which attracts younger individuals and families to these areas, subsequently leading to a higher number of births.

In contrast, many established eastern suburbs are experiencing a natural decrease. This trend may correlate with the significant rise in house prices in these desirable areas, potentially making them less accessible for young, growing families.

A Decade of Change: Comparing 2013 to 2023

Comparing the 2023 map to a decade prior reveals a significant demographic shift across the state. Many regional areas in the east, west, and north of Victoria that showed natural growth (blue) in 2013 have now shifted to a natural decrease (red).

This trend is particularly evident in the Mornington Peninsula. In 2013, it recorded 1,513 births and 1,294 deaths, resulting in a natural increase. By 2023, the number of births had fallen by approximately 15.1% to 1,284, while the number of deaths rose by about 30.4% to 1,687, causing a significant natural decrease.

Notably, almost no regions that were in a state of natural decrease (red) in 2013 have reversed this trend to become areas of natural growth (blue) by 2023. Furthermore, while the Melbourne metropolitan area was predominantly dark blue in 2013 with no areas of strong decline, the 2023 map shows the emergence of dark red areas, indicating that population aging is now a factor even within the metro boundary.
